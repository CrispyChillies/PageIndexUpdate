2.1 Backbone Selection
During the backbone selection stage, we evaluated both CNN-based retrieval encoders
(DenseNet121 [8], ResNet50 [6], ConvNeXtV2) and Vision Transformer (ViT) archi-
tectures (MedSigLIP [18] and MedCLIP [16]) to analyze their compatibility with med-
ical image retrieval. Our initial hypothesis was that medical ViT-based models would
perform better due to their pretraining on paired medical image–text datasets.
However, the empirical results in Table 1 show that CNN-based architectures con-
sistently outperform ViT-based models across all datasets, with ConvNeXtV2 achieving
the best performance in terms of Recall@1 and mAP. This observation reveals an in-
teresting paradox: despite being pretrained on large-scale medical image–text corpora,
ViT-based models such as MedSigLIP and MedCLIP perform worse on pure visual
retrieval tasks. One explanation is that these Transformer-based models are primarily
optimized for cross-modal semantic alignment rather than instance-level visual similar-
ity. Their representations tend to capture high-level semantic concepts but may over-
look subtle structural patterns and fine-grained textures that are crucial in radiological
images. Medical retrieval tasks often depend on small pathological cues such as faint
opacities or local tissue irregularities, which require strong spatial sensitivity.

2.2 Ensemble-Based Multi-View Integration
In clinical radiography, diagnostic consensus is seldom reached through a single per-
spective. Radiologists often analyze medical images from multiple "views," where one
specialist might focus on subtle structural anomalies (Lesion A) while another identifies
broader pathological patterns (Lesion B). To replicate this collaborative expertise, we
propose a Multi-View Ensemble framework utilizing a dual-branch architecture
Our framework utilizes a dual-branch ensemble: a Canonical Branch that extracts
raw features to maintain global data integrity, and an Attentive Refinement Branch inte-
grating Spatial Residual Attention (SRA) to highlight subtle spatial nuances. By fusing
these complementary views, the model captures both fundamental representations and
refined diagnostic cues, ensuring a more robust and holistic interpretation of radio-
graphic data.

a held-out tuning set.
2.3 Large-Scale Indexing with FAISS
To ensure the scalability of our framework in real-world clinical environments, we uti-
lize the FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) library [4] for efficient vector index-
ing. Given the high dimensionality of our ensemble embeddings (2C), we implement
an optimized index structure that supports fast nearest-neighbor search. Specifically,
the gallery embeddings are stored in a flat index (IndexFlatIP) to maintain maxi-
mum retrieval precision via Inner Product (which is equivalent to cosine similarity for
ℓ2-normalized vectors). This indexing pipeline allows the system to query thousands of
historical radiographs in milliseconds, meeting the performance requirements of time-
sensitive clinical decision support.

4 Discussion
We investigated the effectiveness of different backbone architectures for medical image
retrieval and proposed a Multi-View Ensemble with Spatial Residual Attention frame-
work. The key idea is to combine complementary representations from two perspec-
tives: one backbone captures global semantic structures, while the other focuses on
localized lesion patterns that are critical for medical diagnosis. By integrating these
representations, the ensemble improves retrieval robustness compared to using a single
backbone.
To enhance interpretability, we introduce a Concept-to-Region Alignment for Re-
trieval (CRAR) mechanism. Rather than relying solely on traditional saliency maps,
CRAR aligns visually important regions in the query image with similar pathological
patterns in retrieved cases. This provides more informative explanations for similarity-
based retrieval and may help clinicians better understand the reasoning behind the re-
trieved results.
Our experiments also show that conventional convolutional neural network archi-
tectures, particularly ConvNeXtV2, remain highly competitive for medical image re-
trieval. When fine-tuned on domain-specific datasets, these models can outperform sev-
eral biomedical foundation models. This suggests that medical image retrieval relies
strongly on fine-grained texture patterns, which are effectively captured by convolu-
tional architectures.
5 Conclusion
In this work, we demonstrated that combining complementary feature representations
can improve the robustness of medical image retrieval systems across multiple datasets.

2.2 Ensemble-Based Multi-View Integration
In clinical radiography, diagnostic consensus is seldom reached through a single per-
spective. Radiologists often analyze medical images from multiple "views," where one
specialist might focus on subtle structural anomalies (Lesion A) while another identifies
broader pathological patterns (Lesion B). To replicate this collaborative expertise, we
propose a Multi-View Ensemble framework utilizing a dual-branch architecture
Our framework utilizes a dual-branch ensemble: a Canonical Branch that extracts
raw features to maintain global data integrity, and an Attentive Refinement Branch inte-
grating Spatial Residual Attention (SRA) to highlight subtle spatial nuances. By fusing
these complementary views, the model captures both fundamental representations and
refined diagnostic cues, ensuring a more robust and holistic interpretation of radio-
graphic data.

before fusion. The weight pair that maximizes the mean Average Precision (mAP) un-

the weight allocation reflects each model’s actual contribution to retrieval quality un-
der realistic inference conditions, without requiring additional learnable parameters or
a held-out tuning set.
2.3 Large-Scale Indexing with FAISS
To ensure the scalability of our framework in real-world clinical environments, we uti-
lize the FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) library [4] for efficient vector index-
ing. Given the high dimensionality of our ensemble embeddings (2C), we implement
an optimized index structure that supports fast nearest-neighbor search. Specifically,
the gallery embeddings are stored in a flat index (IndexFlatIP) to maintain maxi-
mum retrieval precision via Inner Product (which is equivalent to cosine similarity for
ℓ2-normalized vectors). This indexing pipeline allows the system to query thousands of
historical radiographs in milliseconds, meeting the performance requirements of time-
sensitive clinical decision support.

3.3 Texture-Saliency Paradox Explain
As discussed above, although Vision Transformer (ViT)-based models such as Med-
SigLIP are pretrained on large-scale medical datasets, our experiments show that their
embeddings are not necessarily optimal for medical image retrieval. We observe a dis-
crepancy between retrieval performance and the spatial evidence highlighted by the

model, which we refer to as thetexture–saliency paradox. One explanation lies in the ar-
chitectural differences between CNN-based encoders and ViT-style architectures. ViTs
rely on patch tokenization and self-attention to capture high-level semantic concepts,
but this design may weaken the preservation of continuous spatial structures. In chest
radiography, pathological cues often appear as subtle textures or diffuse patterns across
anatomical regions. Due to the lack of strong local inductive bias, ViT embeddings may
fail to consistently capture these structural signals, whereas convolutional architectures
preserve spatial locality and are therefore better suited for modeling anatomical struc-
tures and texture patterns relevant for retrieval.

4 Discussion
We investigated the effectiveness of different backbone architectures for medical image
retrieval and proposed a Multi-View Ensemble with Spatial Residual Attention frame-
work. The key idea is to combine complementary representations from two perspec-
tives: one backbone captures global semantic structures, while the other focuses on
localized lesion patterns that are critical for medical diagnosis. By integrating these
representations, the ensemble improves retrieval robustness compared to using a single
backbone.
To enhance interpretability, we introduce a Concept-to-Region Alignment for Re-
trieval (CRAR) mechanism. Rather than relying solely on traditional saliency maps,
CRAR aligns visually important regions in the query image with similar pathological
patterns in retrieved cases. This provides more informative explanations for similarity-
based retrieval and may help clinicians better understand the reasoning behind the re-
trieved results.
Our experiments also show that conventional convolutional neural network archi-
tectures, particularly ConvNeXtV2, remain highly competitive for medical image re-
trieval. When fine-tuned on domain-specific datasets, these models can outperform sev-
eral biomedical foundation models. This suggests that medical image retrieval relies
strongly on fine-grained texture patterns, which are effectively captured by convolu-
tional architectures.
5 Conclusion
In this work, we demonstrated that combining complementary feature representations
can improve the robustness of medical image retrieval systems across multiple datasets.
